\chapter{Monte Carlo Robustness Analysis}

The previous chapter showed that simplified methods can approach DIIM at specific $q(0)$ points but not universally. This chapter uses Monte Carlo simulation to systematically evaluate the robustness of simplified methods across a range of realistic disruption profiles and intervention scales.

\section{Framework}

\subsection{Motivation}

All simplified methods produce a \emph{fixed} sector ranking---they depend only on the IO table structure ($A$, $x$), not on the disruption profile ($q(0)$). The DIIM, by contrast, adapts its ranking to the specific $q(0)$. A simplified method performs well when its fixed ranking happens to align with where $q(0)$ concentrates; it fails when the disruption hits sectors that the simplified method did not prioritise.

This insight suggests that the effectiveness of a simplified method is predictable from (i)~features of $q(0)$ and (ii)~the number of sectors targeted for intervention~$k$. At small~$k$, only the very top sectors matter, while at large~$k$ the specific ranking becomes less consequential because most sectors are covered regardless.

\subsection{Performance Metric}

For each simplified method~$m$ and a given $q(0)$, we compute the performance ratio from Equation~\eqref{eq:performance-ratio}. The mean ratio $\bar{R}_m$ across trials is the primary metric: it captures how much of the DIIM's loss reduction a simplified method achieves on average. As a secondary metric, we report the \emph{close rate}---the fraction of trials where $R_m \geq 0.80$, i.e., the simplified method captures at least 80\% of the DIIM's benefit.


\section{Monte Carlo Design}

\subsection{Perturbation of Real $q(0)$}

Rather than drawing $q(0)$ from a uniform distribution---which would generate many unrealistic disruption profiles---we perturb the actual scenario-specific $q(0)$ vector using log-normal noise:

\begin{equation}
    q_i(0)^{\text{trial}} = q_i(0)^{\text{actual}} \cdot \exp\!\bigl(\varepsilon_i\bigr), \quad \varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; 0.5^2)
    \label{eq:q0-perturbation}
\end{equation}

\noindent with the constraint $q_i(0)^{\text{trial}} \in [10^{-6},\; 1]$. This preserves the realistic structure of the disruption (sectors that were heavily impacted remain relatively more impacted) while introducing meaningful variation. The log-normal noise ensures that the perturbation is multiplicative---a sector with $q(0) = 0.01$ can plausibly move to $0.005$ or $0.02$, but not jump to $0.5$.

\subsection{Simulation Procedure}

For each scenario and each value of $k \in \{3, 5, 7, 10, 12\}$, we draw 2\,000 perturbed $q(0)$ vectors. For each trial:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Perturb $q(0)^{\text{actual}}$ using Equation~\eqref{eq:q0-perturbation}.
    \item Run the DIIM baseline (no intervention) and compute total economic loss.
    \item Run the DIIM with intervention in the DIIM's top-$k$ sectors (10\% $q(0)$ reduction) and compute $\Delta EL_{\text{DIIM}}$.
    \item For each simplified method~$m$, run the DIIM with intervention in method~$m$'s top-$k$ sectors and compute $\Delta EL_m$.
    \item Compute $R_m = \Delta EL_m / \Delta EL_{\text{DIIM}}$ and record whether $R_m \geq 0.80$ (``close'').
\end{enumerate}

This yields 20\,000 simulation trials per scenario (2\,000 $\times$ 5 values of $k$), for a total of 40\,000 trials across both scenarios.

\subsection{Three Regimes of $k$}

The results show a non-monotonic pattern that can be decomposed into three regimes:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Small $k$ ($k = 3$)}: All simplified methods agree on the top 3 sectors and produce identical performance with $\bar{R}_m = 0.870$ (COVID-19) and $0.652$ (Manpower). The dominant economic sectors (Manufacturing, Wholesale \& Retail, Transportation \& Storage) are so large that every method---whether based on size, linkage, or centrality---selects them. Two-thirds of trials achieve $\geq$80\% of DIIM in COVID-19 (66.1\%), but only 16.4\% in Manpower. Structural distinctions between simplified methods are irrelevant at this scale.

    \item \textbf{Medium $k$ ($k = 5$--$7$)}: The methods diverge, and structural information adds substantial value. At $k = 5$ in COVID-19, PCA $\times\, x_i$ achieves $\bar{R}_m = 0.817$ versus $0.641$ for Total Output---a 27\% relative improvement---and a close rate of 64.1\% versus just 5.7\%. This dramatic gap arises because PCA selects sector~3 (Construction) over sector~7 (Information \& Communications). At $k = 7$, BL $\times\, x_i$ leads with $\bar{R}_m = 0.834$ and close rate of 67.2\%, capturing sector~12 (Public Administration) via its backward linkage signal. This is the regime where the ``marginal'' sectors (positions 4--7 in the ranking) matter most, and each method's structural lens produces meaningfully different selections.

    \item \textbf{Large $k$ ($k = 10$--$12$)}: The methods converge again as the intervention covers most of the economy. At $k = 12$ (80\% of 15 sectors), close rates reach 100\% for both scenarios, with $\bar{R}_m$ of 0.92--0.98. When intervening on nearly all sectors, the only question is which 3 sectors to \emph{exclude}, and even suboptimal exclusions have limited impact.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Key Observations}

The multi-$k$ tables show several patterns:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{PCA $\times\, x_i$ outperforms Total Output at $k = 5$ (COVID-19)}: PCA $\times\, x_i$ achieves $\bar{R}_m = 0.817$ versus $0.641$ for Total Output---a 27\% relative improvement---and a close rate of 64.1\% versus just 5.7\%. This stems from PCA's inclusion of sector~3 (Construction), which has the highest structural distance ($d_3^{\text{PCA}} = 0.789$) despite ranking only 6th in economic size. Construction's high initial inoperability ($q_3(0) = 0.221$) makes intervening there disproportionately effective.

    \item \textbf{BL $\times\, x_i$ leads at $k = 7$ (COVID-19)}: BL $\times\, x_i$ achieves $\bar{R}_m = 0.834$ and a close rate of 67.2\%, outperforming all other simplified methods including PCA $\times\, x_i$ (0.818, 62.0\%). Its backward linkage signal captures sector~12 (Public Administration), whose strong input dependencies amplify the cascading effect of intervention. This shows that different structural methods have complementary strengths at different intervention scales.

    \item \textbf{PageRank $\times\, x_i$ underperforms at medium $k$}: At $k = 7$, PageRank $\times\, x_i$ drops to $\bar{R}_m = 0.703$ (COVID-19) and $0.749$ (Manpower), with close rates of just 17.7\% and 33.6\% respectively---far below the other methods. PageRank's random-walk centrality prioritises sector~12 over sectors~3 and~10 at positions~6--7, which proves suboptimal under perturbed $q(0)$.

    \item \textbf{All methods converge at large $k$}: At $k = 10$--$12$, close rates reach 97--100\% for most methods, with $\bar{R}_m$ of 0.91--0.98. The specific ranking matters little when the majority of sectors are covered.

    \item \textbf{No single simplified method dominates across all $k$}: PCA $\times\, x_i$ is best at $k = 5$, BL $\times\, x_i$ is best at $k = 7$, and Total Output matches the best at $k = 3$, $10$, and $12$. This suggests that the optimal simplified method depends on the intervention scale.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Practical Implications}

The multi-$k$ analysis leads to the following practical guidelines:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Large-scale intervention ($k \geq 10$)}: Simplified methods are reliable substitutes for DIIM. With $\bar{R}_m$ above 0.90 and close rates of 97--100\%, the additional computational cost of DIIM is difficult to justify when intervening on the majority of sectors. Total Output alone---requiring no IO table analysis---achieves 95\% of DIIM's performance at $k = 12$.

    \item \textbf{Targeted intervention ($k = 3$--$7$)}: DIIM provides substantial value over purely size-based methods. At $k = 5$, Total Output achieves only 64\% of DIIM's loss reduction (close rate 5.7\%). However, structural methods significantly narrow this gap: PCA $\times\, x_i$ achieves 82\% of DIIM (close rate 64.1\%), and BL $\times\, x_i$ leads at $k = 7$ with 83\% of DIIM (close rate 67.2\%). While a gap to DIIM remains, structural methods provide a meaningful improvement over the naive size-based baseline in this regime.

    \item \textbf{Pre-event planning}: When $q(0)$ is unknown, simplified methods remain valuable for pre-positioning resources. The Total Output ranking identifies the sectors whose disruption would cause the greatest absolute economic loss, while PCA $\times\, x_i$ and BL $\times\, x_i$ can flag structurally important sectors that size alone would miss (e.g., Construction in the COVID-19 scenario).

    \item \textbf{Crisis response}: Once $q(0)$ is observed, run the full DIIM if targeting fewer than 10 sectors. The computational cost of the simulation is modest (seconds on modern hardware) and the performance gain is substantial.
\end{enumerate}
