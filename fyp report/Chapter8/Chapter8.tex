\chapter{Conclusion}

This project developed and applied a quantitative framework for assessing economic resilience under disruption, with direct relevance to Singapore's national security planning.

Using the Dynamic Inoperability Input-Output Model (DIIM), two disruption scenarios were studied. The COVID-19 pandemic scenario (15~sectors, 2019~IO table) demonstrated how lockdown-induced workforce unavailability cascaded through input-output linkages to produce sector-level inoperability paths and cumulative economic losses. The manpower disruption scenario (15~sectors, 2022~IO table), motivated by the risk of grey zone conflicts triggering foreign worker departures, revealed how purely structural disruption ($c^* = 0$) produces different cascading dynamics compared to a combined supply-and-demand shock.

A central contribution of this study is the systematic comparison of five simplified methods for identifying key sectors against the DIIM benchmark, evaluated across multiple intervention scales ($k = 3, 5, 7, 10, 12$) using 40\,000 Monte Carlo trials with realistic $q(0)$ perturbations. The analysis reveals three distinct regimes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item At \textbf{small $k$} ($k = 3$), all simplified methods agree on the dominant sectors and perform identically, achieving mean performance ratios of 0.65--0.87.

    \item At \textbf{medium $k$} ($k = 5$--$7$), methods diverge and structural information adds substantial value. PCA $\times\, x_i$ outperforms the Total Output baseline by up to 27\% in the COVID-19 scenario (close rate 64\% vs 6\%) by identifying structurally critical sectors (e.g., Construction) that size-based rankings miss. BL $\times\, x_i$ provides complementary value at $k = 7$ (close rate 67\%). While the best simplified method still achieves only 82\% of DIIM's mean performance at $k = 5$, structural methods dramatically narrow the gap compared to the naive size-based baseline.

    \item At \textbf{large $k$} ($k \geq 10$), simplified methods converge to DIIM performance, with mean ratios of 0.91--0.98 and close rates of 97--100\%. At this scale, even the simplest method---ranking by Total Output alone---is a reliable substitute for the full simulation.
\end{itemize}

The remarkable equivalence between Total Output and structurally-weighted methods at most $k$ values underscores that a sector's absolute economic scale is the dominant predictor of macro-level loss reduction. Structural analysis adds value only in the medium-$k$ regime, where the marginal sectors differ across methods, and only when the economy contains sectors with a significant mismatch between economic size and structural importance.

Simplified methods perform best when the disruption profile aligns with the economic size distribution---a condition that is scenario-dependent and cannot be assumed in advance.

For national security planning, this enables a scale-dependent approach: use simplified structural analysis for peacetime resilience investments and for large-scale crisis response ($k \geq 10$), but run the full DIIM when targeting fewer than 10 sectors during a crisis. The computational cost of the DIIM is modest, and the performance gain at small $k$ is substantial.

The framework is generalisable beyond the two scenarios studied here. Any economy with an available IO table can compute the simplified structural rankings in peacetime and apply the multi-$k$ analysis to determine the appropriate tool for its intervention scale. Future work could extend the approach to finer sector disaggregation, correlated disruptions, and multi-period dynamics.
