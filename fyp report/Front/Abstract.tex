\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This project develops a framework for assessing economic resilience under disruption using the Dynamic Inoperability Input-Output Model (DIIM) \cite{SantosHaimes2004,JinZhou2023}. The DIIM captures how sectoral disruptions propagate through input-output linkages and how the economy recovers over time. Two disruption scenarios relevant to Singapore are studied: (1)~the COVID-19 pandemic, using the 2019 input-output (IO) table aggregated to 15~sectors, and (2)~a manpower disruption scenario motivated by potential grey zone conflicts that could trigger large-scale foreign worker departures, using the 2022 IO table with 15~sectors.

In disruption management, a key question is which sectors should receive priority intervention. The DIIM can identify these ``key sectors'' by simulating the full disruption and ranking sectors by economic loss, but this approach is computationally expensive and requires crisis-time data (initial inoperability~$q(0)$, demand perturbation~$c^{*}$) that may not be available during the early stages of an event. This motivates the search for simplified alternatives that use only the IO table.

This study evaluates five simplified methods against the DIIM benchmark through Monte Carlo simulation. Performance is measured by the ratio of economic loss reduction achieved by each simplified method relative to the DIIM benchmark. For the 15-sector COVID scenario, the best structural methods (Total Output, PageRank $\times\, x_i$, BL $\times\, x_i$, and FL $\times\, x_i$) match DIIM in 89.8\% of cases, and PCA $\times\, x_i$ matches in 86.6\%. For the 15-sector manpower scenario, FL $\times\, x_i$ and Total Output match in 55.4\% of cases, while PCA $\times\, x_i$ drops to 38.8\%.
