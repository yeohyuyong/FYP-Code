\chapter{Case Study 2: Manpower Disruption}

This chapter applies the DIIM framework to a manpower disruption scenario motivated by Singapore's dependence on foreign labour and the potential for grey zone conflicts to trigger workforce departures.

\section{Background: Singapore's Foreign Worker Dependence}

Singapore's economy relies on approximately 1.4~million foreign workers, roughly one-third of the total labour force \cite{MOM2023ForeignWorkforce}. These workers are concentrated in construction, manufacturing, marine shipyard, process industries, and domestic services. This reliance on foreign labour, while supporting economic growth, creates a vulnerability to events that could trigger rapid workforce departures.

Grey zone conflicts, coercive actions that fall below the threshold of conventional warfare, represent a plausible threat scenario \cite{MINDEF2020Factsheet}. Economic sanctions, cyber attacks on critical infrastructure, disinformation campaigns, or military posturing could erode confidence among foreign workers and their home governments, prompting rapid departures.

Unlike the COVID-19 scenario, where disruption arose from both workforce unavailability ($q(0) > 0$) and reduced demand ($c^{*} > 0$), a manpower disruption is characterised by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High initial inoperability} ($q(0) > 0$): Sectors lose productive capacity as workers depart.
    \item \textbf{No external demand shock} ($c^* = 0$): The disruption is purely structural. Consumer and government demand for goods and services is assumed to persist at pre-disruption levels; sectors simply cannot fulfil that demand due to workforce shortages.
\end{itemize}

With $c^* = 0$, the DIIM solution simplifies to $q(t) = e^{-K(I-A^{*})t}\, q(0)$ (Equation~\eqref{eq:diim-solution-homogeneous}), meaning the disruption is governed entirely by the structure of the economy ($A^{*}$) and the initial shock ($q(0)$).


\section{Data and Model Parameters}

\subsection{Input-Output Table}

This scenario uses the 2022 Singapore IO table, aggregated to the same 15~broad sectors as the COVID-19 scenario. This allows direct comparison between the two case studies.

\subsection{Initial Inoperability}

Initial inoperability is computed using the same method as Equation~\eqref{eq:initial-inoperability}, with workforce unavailability data reflecting the proportion of foreign workers in each sector. Sectors with high foreign worker concentrations (e.g., construction, admin & support) have correspondingly higher initial inoperability.

\subsection{Model Parameters}

Since we are modelling the manpower disruption with no shock duration, $c^* = 0$. The simulation runs for 751~days with a final inoperability of 1\% of the initial shock ($q(0)$), the same used in the COVID-19 scenario. The COVID-19 scenario uses 366~days per year (2020 being a leap year), while the manpower scenario uses 365~days per year (2022).
