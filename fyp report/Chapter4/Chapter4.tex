\chapter{Case Study 2: Manpower Disruption}

This chapter applies the DIIM framework to a manpower disruption scenario motivated by Singapore's dependence on foreign labour and the potential for grey zone conflicts to trigger workforce departures.

\section{Background: Singapore's Foreign Worker Dependence}

Singapore's economy relies on approximately 1.4~million foreign workers, roughly one-third of the total labour force \cite{MOM2023ForeignWorkforce}. These workers are concentrated in construction, manufacturing, marine shipyard, process industries, and domestic services. This reliance on foreign labour, while supporting economic growth, creates a structural vulnerability to events that could trigger rapid workforce departures.

Grey zone conflicts, coercive actions that fall below the threshold of conventional warfare, represent a plausible threat scenario \cite{MINDEF2020Factsheet}. Economic sanctions, cyber attacks on critical infrastructure, disinformation campaigns, or military posturing could erode confidence among foreign workers and their home governments, prompting rapid departures.

Unlike the COVID-19 scenario, where disruption arose from both workforce unavailability ($q(0) > 0$) and reduced demand ($c^{*} > 0$), a manpower disruption is characterised by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High initial inoperability} ($q(0) > 0$): Sectors lose productive capacity as workers depart.
    \item \textbf{No external demand shock} ($c^* = 0$): The disruption is purely structural. Consumer and government demand for goods and services is assumed to persist at pre-disruption levels; sectors simply cannot fulfil that demand due to workforce shortages.
\end{itemize}

This distinction follows from the DIIM's separation of supply-side and demand-side disruption channels. In the model, $q(0)$ captures the initial loss of production capacity (supply-side), while $c^*$ captures a reduction in final demand (demand-side). Because workforce departures degrade \emph{production capacity} without reducing end-user demand, the manpower scenario maps entirely to $q(0)$, with $c^* = 0$.

With $c^* = 0$, the DIIM solution simplifies to $q(t) = e^{-K(I-A^{*})t}\, q(0)$ (Equation~\eqref{eq:diim-solution-homogeneous}), meaning the disruption dynamics are governed entirely by the structure of the economy ($A^{*}$) and the initial shock ($q(0)$).


\section{Data and Model Parameters}

\subsection{Input-Output Table}

This scenario uses the 2022 Singapore IO table, aggregated to the same 15~broad sectors as the COVID-19 scenario. This common classification allows direct comparison between the two case studies.

\subsection{Initial Inoperability}

Initial inoperability is computed using the same method as Equation~\eqref{eq:initial-inoperability}, with workforce unavailability data reflecting the proportion of foreign workers in each sector. Sectors with high foreign worker concentrations (e.g., construction, process industries) have correspondingly higher initial inoperability.

\subsection{Model Parameters}

The shock duration parameter is set to 55~days for consistency with the COVID-19 scenario, though because $c^* = 0$ here, the shock duration has no effect on the simulation: the $c^*$ term that would be removed after the shock period is already zero throughout. The total simulation horizon is 751~days with a final inoperability of 1\%, representing near-full recovery through workforce replacement, retraining, or automation. This horizon matches the COVID-19 scenario for comparability; in practice, recovery may be shorter or longer depending on the ease of workforce replacement. The COVID-19 scenario uses 366~days per year (2020 being a leap year), while the manpower scenario uses 365~days per year (2022).


\section{Results}

\subsection{Inoperability Evolution}

Figure~\ref{fig:manpower-inoperability} shows the simulated inoperability trajectories for the 15~sectors. Compared to the COVID-19 scenario, the manpower disruption exhibits:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Greater dispersion in initial inoperability across sectors, reflecting the uneven distribution of foreign workers.
    \item Cascading effects driven entirely by the production network, as disruptions in labour-intensive sectors propagate to downstream industries without any demand shock.
\end{itemize}
