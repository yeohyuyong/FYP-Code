\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background}

Singapore is a small, open economy whose prosperity depends on deep integration with global supply chains and a large foreign workforce. Approximately one-third of Singapore's labour force consists of non-resident workers, spanning construction, manufacturing, marine shipyard, and services \cite{MOM2023ForeignWorkforce}. This dependence is also a vulnerability as any event that rapidly reduces the available workforce, be it a pandemic or geopolitical crisis, can propagate through interconnected sectors and cause cascading economic losses beyond the initially affected industries.

The COVID-19 pandemic illustrated this. When Singapore imposed the ``circuit breaker'' lockdown from 7~April to 1~June~2020 \cite{MOH2020EndCircuitBreaker,MOH2020PostCircuitBreakerMeasures}, the disruption was not confined to directly affected sectors. Construction shutdowns deprived building-material manufacturers of demand, transport restrictions disrupted logistics, and hospitality closures cascaded into food supply chains. Understanding these cascading effects requires a framework that captures inter-sectoral dependencies and how disruptions propagate over time.

Beyond pandemics, Singapore faces national security scenarios that could produce similar or more severe manpower disruptions. Grey zone conflicts, such as economic coercion, cyber attacks, or hybrid threats below the threshold of conventional warfare, could trigger rapid departures of foreign workers from sectors that rely heavily on non-resident labour \cite{MINDEF2020Factsheet}. Unlike COVID-19, where the external demand shock ($c^{*}$) was significant, a manpower disruption is characterised by high initial inoperability ($q(0)$) but with $c^{*}=0$. It is a purely structural disruption that propagates through the network.

\section{The Dynamic Inoperability Input-Output Model}

The Inoperability Input-Output Model (IIM) and its dynamic extension (DIIM) provide a framework for modelling such disruptions \cite{SantosHaimes2004}. Built on the Leontief input-output model, the DIIM measures ``inoperability'', the normalised production loss of each sector on a scale from~0 (normal) to~1 (complete shutdown), and simulates how initial shocks propagate through input-output linkages and how the economy recovers over time. The Shanghai COVID-19 study demonstrated this approach for quantifying sectoral vulnerability and evaluating risk management strategies \cite{JinZhou2023}.

\section{The Key Sector Identification Problem}

One application of the DIIM is identifying ``key sectors'' where targeted intervention (e.g., reducing inoperability by allocating recovery resources) yields the greatest reduction in total economic loss. The DIIM identifies these sectors by running the full simulation and ranking sectors by cumulative economic loss. However, one limitation of this approach is that the DIIM needs crisis-time data, specifically initial inoperability $q(0)$ and the demand perturbation $c^{*}$, which may not be available early in a disruption or during pre-event planning.

This raises the question of whether there exist simpler methods that can identify key sectors using only the input-output table, which is readily available.

\section{Objectives}

This study addresses the following objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Apply the DIIM framework to two disruption scenarios relevant to Singapore: the COVID-19 pandemic (15~sectors, 2019 IO table) and a manpower disruption (15~sectors, 2022 IO table).
    \item Evaluate five simplified methods for key sector identification, namely Total Output ($x_i$), Principal Component Analysis (PCA) $\times\, x_i$, PageRank $\times\, x_i$, Backward Linkage (BL) $\times\, x_i$, and Forward Linkage (FL) $\times\, x_i$, against the DIIM benchmark.
    \item Determine how the performance gap between simplified methods and DIIM varies with the number of sectors targeted for intervention ($k$), identifying the conditions where simplified methods are sufficient and where DIIM remains necessary.
\end{enumerate}
