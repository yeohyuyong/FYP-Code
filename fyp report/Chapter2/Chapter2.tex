\chapter{Methodology}

This chapter presents the analytical framework. Section~\ref{sec:diim-derivation} derives the DIIM from the Leontief input-output model. Section~\ref{sec:simplified-methods} describes the key sector identification methods. Section~\ref{sec:performance-metric} defines the performance metric used to compare methods.

\section{The Dynamic Inoperability Input-Output Model}
\label{sec:diim-derivation}

\subsection{Inoperability}

This paper uses the DIIM to assess the impact of economic disruptions on sectoral inoperability and economic losses in Singapore. The DIIM is derived from the Leontief input-output model and captures how shocks propagate through inter-sector linkages. Inoperability measures how far a sector's output has fallen from its planned level:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:inoperability-definition}
    \text{Inoperability} = \frac{\text{As Planned Production} - \text{Degraded Production}}{\text{As Planned Production}}
\end{equation}

Where ``As Planned Production'' is the output level under normal conditions, and ``Degraded Production'' is the output level after a disruption. Inoperability ranges from 0 to 1. A value of 0 means the sector is operating normally, while a value of 1 means production has stopped entirely.

\subsection{From Leontief to IIM}

The IIM can be derived from the Leontief input-output model \cite{Leontief1936}, shown in Equation~\eqref{eq:leontief-model}.

\begin{equation}
    x = A x + c
    \label{eq:leontief-model}
\end{equation}

Where $x$ is the total output vector; $A$ is the technical coefficient matrix; $c$ is the final demand vector. After a disruption, the output levels and final demand shift to $\tilde{x}$ and $\tilde{c}$ respectively.

Subtracting Equation~\eqref{eq:leontief-model} from the disrupted-economy variables:

\begin{equation}
    x - \tilde{x} = A (x - \tilde{x}) + (c - \tilde{c})
    \label{eq:leontief-difference}
\end{equation}

To normalise each sector's output loss by its planned output, both sides of Equation~\eqref{eq:leontief-difference} are left-multiplied by $\hat{x}^{-1}$, where $\hat{x}$ is the diagonal matrix of the output vector:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{x}^{-1}(x - \tilde{x}) = \hat{x}^{-1}A(x - \tilde{x}) + \hat{x}^{-1}(c - \tilde{c})
    \label{eq:normalized-difference}
\end{equation}

Let $q = \hat{x}^{-1}\left(x - \tilde{x}\right)$, $A^{*} = \hat{x}^{-1} A \hat{x}$, and $c^{*} = \hat{x}^{-1}\left(c - \tilde{c}\right)$. Substituting these into Equation~\eqref{eq:normalized-difference} gives the IIM:

\begin{equation}
    q = A^{*} q + c^{*}
    \label{eq:iim-model}
\end{equation}

The details of each matrix in the IIM model are shown in Equations~\eqref{eq:q-definition}, \eqref{eq:astar-definition}, and~\eqref{eq:cstar-definition}:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:q-definition}
    q = \hat{x}^{-1} (x - \tilde{x}) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \dfrac{1}{x_1} & 0 & \cdots & \cdots & 0 \\
        0 & \ddots & & & \vdots \\
        \vdots & & \dfrac{1}{x_i} & & \vdots \\
        \vdots & & & \ddots & 0 \\
        0 & \cdots & \cdots & 0 & \dfrac{1}{x_n}
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        x_1 - \tilde{x}_1 \\
        \vdots \\
        x_i - \tilde{x}_i \\
        \vdots \\
        x_n - \tilde{x}_n
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    A^{*} = \hat{x}^{-1} A \hat{x} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        a_{11}\dfrac{x_{1}}{x_{1}} & \cdots & a_{1j}\dfrac{x_{j}}{x_{1}} & \cdots & a_{1n}\dfrac{x_{n}}{x_{1}} \\
        \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
        a_{i1}\dfrac{x_{1}}{x_{i}} & \cdots & a_{ij}\dfrac{x_{j}}{x_{i}} & \cdots & a_{in}\dfrac{x_{n}}{x_{i}} \\
        \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
        a_{n1}\dfrac{x_{1}}{x_{n}} & \cdots & a_{nj}\dfrac{x_{j}}{x_{n}} & \cdots & a_{nn}\dfrac{x_{n}}{x_{n}}
    \end{bmatrix}
    \label{eq:astar-definition}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:cstar-definition}
    \mathbf{c}^{*} = \hat{\mathbf{x}}^{-1} \left( \mathbf{c} - \tilde{\mathbf{c}} \right)
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \dfrac{1}{x_{1}} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
        0 & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\
        \vdots & \ddots & \dfrac{1}{x_{i}} & 0 \\
        \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \\
        0 & \cdots & \cdots & \dfrac{1}{x_{n}}
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        c_{1} - \tilde{c}_{1} \\
        \vdots \\
        c_{i} - \tilde{c}_{i} \\
        \vdots \\
        c_{n} - \tilde{c}_{n}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The DIIM}

The static IIM can be extended to the DIIM by adding a time variable $t$ and a recovery matrix $K$ that describes how quickly each sector bounces back after a shock \cite{SantosHaimes2004}. In discrete form, the DIIM is:

\begin{equation}
    q(t+1) = q(t) + K \big[ A^{*} q(t) + c^{*}(t) - q(t) \big]
    \label{eq:diim-discrete}
\end{equation}

Where $K$ is a diagonal matrix with positive entries, since each sector's recovery is assumed to depend only on itself, not on its linkages with other sectors. A larger diagonal entry means the sector recovers faster. $t$ is the discrete time variable and $q(t)$ is the sectoral inoperability vector at time~$t$. Another form of Equation~\eqref{eq:diim-discrete} is:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:diim-continuous}
    \dot{q}(t) = K \bigl[ A^{*} q(t) + c^{*}(t) - q(t) \bigr]
\end{equation}

Solving Equation~\eqref{eq:diim-continuous} gives the inoperability over time:

\begin{equation}
    q(t) = e^{-K(I-A^{*})t} q(0) + \int_{0}^{t} K e^{-K(I-A^{*})(t-z)} c^{*}(z)\, dz
    \label{eq:diim-solution-general}
\end{equation}

When there is no demand shock ($c^{*}=0$), this simplifies to:

\begin{equation}
    q(t) = e^{-K (I - A^{*}) t} q(0)
    \label{eq:diim-solution-homogeneous}
\end{equation}

Where $q(0)$ is the initial inoperability vector right after the shock hits. Over time, the inoperability decays at a rate that depends on both how fast sectors recover ($K$) and how strongly sectors are linked ($A^{*}$).

\subsection{Initial Inoperability}

The initial inoperability $q(0)$ is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:initial-inoperability}
    q_i(0) = \underbrace{\left(1 - \text{RLI}_i\right)\left(1 - \text{ESS}_i\right)}_{\text{Workforce Unavailability}} \times \frac{\text{LAPI}_i}{\text{Sector Output}_i}
\end{equation}

The first factor, workforce unavailability, is adapted from \cite{DelRioChanona2020,Pichler2022}. $\text{RLI}_i$ is the Remote Labor Index, representing the probability that a worker in sector~$i$ can work from home. $\text{ESS}_i$ is the Essential Score, representing the probability that a worker has an essential job that permits on-site work during a lockdown. The product $(1-\text{RLI}_i)(1-\text{ESS}_i)$ gives the expected fraction of workers who cannot work during the lockdown.

The second factor, $\text{LAPI}_i / \text{Sector Output}_i$, captures how dependent each sector is on labour as an input, where LAPI is the Local Area Personal Income (equivalent to compensation of employees in the IO table).

\subsection{Elasticity Coefficient}

From Equation~\eqref{eq:diim-solution-homogeneous}, the inoperability of a single sector~$i$ over time follows:

\begin{equation}
    q_i(t) = q_i(0) \, e^{-k_i \left( 1 - a_{ii}^{*} \right) t}
    \label{eq:qi-exponential-decay}
\end{equation}

Rearranging Equation~\eqref{eq:qi-exponential-decay} to solve for~$k_i$:

\begin{equation}
    k_i = \frac{\ln\left[q_i(0)/q_i(T)\right]}{T_i\left(1 - a_{ii}^{*}\right)}
    \label{eq:ki-definition}
\end{equation}

Where $q_i(0)$ is the initial inoperability of sector~$i$; $q_i(T)$ is the inoperability at time~$T$; $a_{ii}^{*}$ is the diagonal entry of $A^{*}$, which captures how much sector~$i$ depends on its own output.

\subsection{Economic Loss}

The economic loss for sector~$i$ over the recovery period is:

\begin{equation}
    EL_i = x_i \int_{t=0}^{t=T} q_i(t) \, dt
    \label{eq:economic-loss}
\end{equation}

Where $x_i$ is the planned output of sector~$i$ under normal conditions.


\section{Key Sector Identification Methods}
\label{sec:simplified-methods}

A key sector is defined as one where targeted intervention, modelled as a 10\% reduction in initial inoperability $q(0)$, produces the largest reduction in total economic loss. The evaluation procedure is described first, then the benchmark and five simplified alternatives.

\subsection{Intervention and Loss Reduction Evaluation}
\label{sec:intervention-procedure}

Each key sector identification method produces a ranking of sectors. To evaluate a method's effectiveness, we select its top-$k$ ranked sectors and simulate a targeted intervention: the initial inoperability of each selected sector is reduced by 10\%. The DIIM is then re-run with this modified $q(0)$ to obtain the post-intervention total economic loss. The difference between the baseline loss (no intervention) and the post-intervention loss gives the economic loss reduction achieved by that method's top-$k$ selection. This procedure is applied identically to the DIIM benchmark and to every simplified method, so that all methods are compared equally.

The formal procedure is given in Algorithm~\ref{alg:loss-reduction}.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Intervention and Loss Reduction Evaluation}\label{alg:loss-reduction}
\KwIn{$q(0)$, $A^*$, $c^*$, $x$, number of key sectors $k$, set of ranking methods $M$}
\KwOut{$\%\Delta EL_m$ for every method $m \in M$}
\BlankLine
Run DIIM with $q(0)$ to obtain baseline loss $EL_{\text{base}}$\;
\BlankLine
\ForEach{method $m \in M$}{
  Rank sectors using method $m$; select top-$k$ set $S_k$\;
  $q'(0) \leftarrow q(0)$\;
  \ForEach{sector $i \in S_k$}{
    $q'_i(0) \leftarrow 0.9 \times q_i(0)$\;
  }
  Re-run DIIM with $q'(0)$ to obtain $EL_{\text{int}}$\;
  $\%\Delta EL_m \leftarrow \dfrac{EL_{\text{base}} - EL_{\text{int}}}{EL_{\text{base}}} \times 100$\;
}
\BlankLine
\Return $\%\Delta EL_m$ for all $m \in M$\;
\end{algorithm}

In words: the DIIM is first run with the original $q(0)$ to establish the baseline loss. A ranking method $m$ then produces an ordered list of sectors, and the top $k$ are selected. Their initial inoperability is reduced by 10\%, representing a targeted intervention that partially restores production capacity at time zero. The DIIM is re-run with this modified $q'(0)$ to obtain the post-intervention loss. The loss reduction is the difference between baseline and post-intervention losses. This procedure is repeated for every method with identical $q(0)$, $k$, and intervention magnitude, so differences in loss reduction reflect only the quality of each method's ranking.

\subsection{DIIM-Based Identification (Benchmark)}

The DIIM identifies key sectors by running the full simulation without intervention, then ranking sectors by their cumulative economic loss $EL_i$ from Equation~\eqref{eq:economic-loss}. The top-$k$ sectors are selected for intervention. This is the benchmark but requires knowledge of $q(0)$ and~$c^{*}$.

\subsection{Total Output ($x_i$)}

Sectors are ranked by their total planned output $x_i$ which is readily available in the input-output table. This method ignores the interdependency structure entirely and assumes that intervening in the largest sectors will yield the greatest loss reduction.

\subsection{PCA $\times\, x_i$}

Rather than using $A$ directly, this method constructs the integrated input-output coefficient matrix:

\begin{equation}
    H = A (I - A)^{-1} = A \cdot L
    \label{eq:integrated-io-matrix}
\end{equation}

where $L = (I - A)^{-1}$ is the Leontief inverse. $H$ captures both direct and indirect effects through the production network. PCA is applied to~$H$, and each sector's distance is computed as its Euclidean distance from the origin in the principal component space:

\begin{equation}
    d_i^{\text{PCA}} = \sqrt{ \sum_{k=1}^{n\_pcs} v_{ik}^2 }
    \label{eq:pca-distance}
\end{equation}

where $v_{ik}$ is the loading of sector~$i$ on principal component $k$. This distance is then weighted by the sector's total output $x_i$:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Score}_i^{\text{PCA}} = d_i^{\text{PCA}} \times x_i
    \label{eq:pca-xi-score}
\end{equation}

Sectors with larger $\text{Score}_i^{\text{PCA}}$ are ranked higher.

\subsection{PageRank $\times\, x_i$}

The interdependency matrix $A^*$ is treated as the weighted adjacency matrix of a directed graph. The PageRank algorithm \cite{BrinPage1998} computes a centrality score $PR_i$ for each sector based on the structure of incoming links. This score is weighted by total output:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Score}_i^{\text{PR}} = PR_i \times x_i
    \label{eq:pagerank-xi}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Backward Linkage $\times\, x_i$ (BL $\times\, x_i$)}

Backward linkages measure the extent to which a sector draws on other sectors' outputs as inputs \cite{Rasmussen1956,Chenery1958}. They are calculated as the column sums of the Leontief inverse:

\begin{equation}
    BL_j = \sum_{i=1}^{n} l_{ij}
    \label{eq:backward-linkage}
\end{equation}

where $l_{ij}$ are elements of $L$. A high $BL_j$ indicates that sector~$j$ draws heavily on other sectors as inputs. This linkage is weighted by total output:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Score}_j^{\text{BL}} = BL_j \times x_j
\end{equation}

\subsection{Forward Linkage $\times\, x_i$ (FL $\times\, x_i$)}

Forward linkages measure how much a sector's output is used as inputs by other sectors \cite{Rasmussen1956,Dietzenbacher2002}. They are calculated as the row sums of the Leontief inverse:

\begin{equation}
    FL_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} l_{ij}
    \label{eq:forward-linkage}
\end{equation}

A high $FL_i$ indicates that sector~$i$ is a major supplier to the rest of the economy. This linkage is weighted by total output:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Score}_i^{\text{FL}} = FL_i \times x_i
\end{equation}

\section{Performance Metric}
\label{sec:performance-metric}

To compare a simplified method against DIIM, we define the \emph{performance ratio}:

\begin{equation}
    R_m = \frac{\Delta EL_m}{\Delta EL_{\text{DIIM}}}
    \label{eq:performance-ratio}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta EL_m$ is the total economic loss reduction achieved by intervening in the top-$k$ sectors identified by simplified method~$m$, and $\Delta EL_{\text{DIIM}}$ is the reduction achieved by the DIIM benchmark's top-$k$ sectors. If $R_m \geq 0.80$, the simplified method is considered ``close enough'' to substitute for DIIM.
