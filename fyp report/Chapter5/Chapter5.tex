\chapter{Key Sector Identification Methods}

This chapter compares the key sector identification methods described in Chapter~2 using the actual disruption data from both case studies. Recall that the DIIM identifies key sectors by running the full simulation and ranking by economic loss (expensive, requires $q(0)$ and $c^{*}$), while the simplified methods use only the IO table structure.

These closely mirror the COVID-19 PCA sectors, reflecting the similar aggregated structure, though the different IO table vintage (2022 vs 2019) and the absence of demand shock ($c^* = 0$) produce some ranking differences.

Several observations emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{High method agreement}: By incorporating total output $x_i$ as a weight, the methods converge strongly. For COVID-19, Total Output, PageRank $\times$ xi, BL $\times$ xi, and FL $\times$ xi produce the exact same top-5 ranking (2, 4, 5, 8, 7). PCA $\times$ xi differs only slightly by including sector 3 instead of 7.
    \item \textbf{Economic size dominates}: The fact that structurally distinct measures (centrality, backward linkage, and forward linkage) yield nearly identical rankings once weighted by $x_i$ indicates that a sector's baseline economic size is the dominant driver of its overall impact in these aggregated models.
    \item \textbf{Cross-scenario consistency}: Despite using different IO tables (2019 vs 2022) and different shock structures ($c^* > 0$ vs $c^* = 0$), both scenarios produce very similar simplified method rankings. The manpower scenario rankings (2, 4/5, 5/4, 7, 8) closely mirror the COVID-19 rankings (2, 4, 5, 8, 7), confirming that economic size is the overwhelmingly dominant factor in these 15-sector models.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Comparison at Original Disruption Data}

Using the actual $q(0)$ data from each scenario, we compare each simplified method's economic loss reduction against the DIIM benchmark when intervening in the top-5 sectors.

For the COVID-19 scenario, the DIIM identifies sectors 2, 3, 4, 5, 12 as key sectors and achieves a loss reduction of 3.91\%. In contrast, Total Output, PageRank $\times$ xi, BL $\times$ xi, and FL $\times$ xi achieve 2.65\%, while PCA $\times$ xi achieves 3.38\%---a notable improvement over the purely size-based methods. The DIIM selects Construction (3) and Public Administration (12) in place of Financial Services (8) and Information \& Communications (7), reflecting its ability to capture sectors with high initial inoperability and strong structural dependencies.

For the manpower scenario, PCA $\times$ xi matches the other simplified methods at 4.84\%, while the DIIM achieves 6.88\%---a substantially larger gap. This indicates that when disruptions are concentrated in structurally important sectors, the DIIM's ability to account for both $q(0)$ and $c^{*}$ becomes more valuable.

This observation motivates the Monte Carlo analysis in Chapter~6: the performance of simplified methods at a single $q(0)$ point is insufficient to draw general conclusions. We need to understand how performance varies across the space of possible $q(0)$ vectors.
